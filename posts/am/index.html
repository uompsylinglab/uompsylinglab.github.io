"Focalization strategies in Reunion Creole: two types of it-cleft?" by Alina McLellan

Posted Feb 20, 2020

Summary This study fills a typological gap in the literature, investigating sé focus constructions in Reunion Creole (RC). It compares the syntax, information structure and discourse functions of these constructions with findings from the cross-linguistic literature on clefts.

Background Clefts are bi-clausal constructions which express a single proposition. The prototypical cleft construction contains an expletive pronoun, copula, clefted element and relative-like clause, as in (1a). Clefts have a simple sentence counterpart (1b) which holds the same truth values (Lambrecht 2001). Lambrecht (2001) argues that clefts exhibit non-canonical syntax in order to change the focus structure of a sentence. The cleft in (1a) places focus on the argument John, which would otherwise be non-focal.

a. It was John that we saw.
b. We saw John.

Data Data is presented from a corpus built upon that gathered for the author’s MA research. Similar to the French c’est-cleft, RC has a focus construction introduced by a highlighter, sé. However, the background clause is not always introduced by a relativiser, as demonstrated below.

Sé la mashine k’ i konvien pa
HL DEF machine REL FIN work NEG
‘It is the machine which doesn’t work’

Sé lo sistèm lé mal roganizé
HL DEF system COP.PRS badly organised
‘It is the system that is badly organised’

Discussion According to Maurer & the APiCS Consortium (2013), creoles focalize noun phrases using a highlighter, which may be a copula or a focus particle. In their terms, (2) and (3) are separate focalization strategies; (2) is considered clefting and (3) focus fronting. To be classified as a cleft, if the focus construction does not contain a copula as its highlighter, it must have a relativiser introducing the background clause. Bollée (2013) analyses sé as a focus particle in RC, therefore (3) would be considered focus fronting. The distribution of the two strategies and the focalization of constituent types other than noun phrases are understudied in RC. This study aims to shed light on rules governing the presence of the relativiser: whether it depends on the type of clefted element, and whether the structures in (2) and (3) are variants of one biclausal construction or different focalization strategies. The cross-linguistic literature finds that adverbial clefts may differ from nominal clefts, in terms of their information structure and discourse functions. In French, it is suggested that adverbial clefts have grammaticalised to some extent, losing some of their focalising function (De Cesare & Garassino 2018). The study compares the information structure and discourse functions of sé focus constructions against these findings.

Preview Preliminary results find sé attested in the past tense as sété (4). I therefore question Bollée’s (2013) analysis of sé as a focus particle rather than a copula. A copula analysis of sé points to a conclusion that (2) and (3) are indeed variants of one biclausal cleft construction. In any case, it appears that the French expletive subject ce and copula forms est/était were re-analysed together as lexicalised forms sé and sété in RC.

Sété lu komsa ke dan larondisman qui faisait les demann
HL 3sg so REL in the.area REL do.IPFV DEF.PL request
‘It was him who, in the area, made the requests’
(Barat et al, 1976)

Adducing primary RC data, the talk contributes to our understanding of the typology of it-clefts in Romance-related creoles and beyond.
